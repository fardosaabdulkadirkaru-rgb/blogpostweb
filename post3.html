Leadership Journey: From Educator to Activist

October 25, 2023

My journey from a dedicated educator to a passionate grassroots activist has been one of transformation, resilience, and unwavering commitment. Born in the Borana community, I began my career in education, believing that knowledge is the key to empowerment. However, as I witnessed the struggles of women and girls around me, I realized that true change requires more than teaching in a classroom—it demands action in the community.

The Early Years as an Educator

As a young teacher, I poured my heart into educating children in remote areas of Northern Kenya. Despite limited resources and challenging conditions, I saw the potential in every student. My experiences opened my eyes to the broader issues affecting our communities, such as gender inequality and lack of access to basic services. These insights sparked a desire to do more than educate; I wanted to advocate for systemic change.

The transition wasn't easy. I faced skepticism from those who believed women should focus on traditional roles. Yet, "Tunaweza!" – We can do it! – became my mantra. I began organizing small community meetings, discussing women's rights and health issues. This grassroots approach laid the foundation for my activism.

Transition to Grassroots Activism

Realizing that individual efforts could create collective impact, I shifted from teaching to full-time activism. I founded MWADO (Marsabit Women Advocacy and Development Organization) in 2010, bringing together women from diverse backgrounds to address local challenges. Our early work focused on raising awareness about female genital mutilation (FGM) and gender-based violence, issues that were often taboo in our communities.

Leadership in MWADO has been both rewarding and demanding. I've learned to navigate cultural sensitivities, build coalitions, and advocate for policy changes. From organizing workshops to lobbying for better healthcare, every step has strengthened my resolve. The support of fellow activists and community members has been invaluable, reminding me that leadership is about lifting others as we rise together.

Key Moments in My Journey

  • 2005: Began teaching and noticed disparities in girls' education.
  • 2008: Started community discussions on women's health and rights.
  • 2010: Founded MWADO, marking my full transition to activism.
  • 2012: Led first anti-FGM campaign in Borana communities.
  • 2015: Expanded MWADO's reach to include economic empowerment programs.
  • 2020: Received national recognition, amplifying our voice on a larger stage.

Challenges and Triumphs

The path hasn't been without obstacles. I've faced resistance from traditional leaders, threats from those opposed to our work, and the emotional toll of advocating for change. Yet, each challenge has forged my leadership. Witnessing women gain confidence, communities thrive, and policies shift has been the greatest reward. My journey teaches that true leadership comes from the heart and is sustained by community support.

Below is a timeline of my leadership journey:

Year Milestone
2000-2005 Educator: Teaching in local schools, building foundation in community service
2005-2010 Emerging Activist: Organizing awareness campaigns on women's issues
2010 Founder: Established MWADO to formalize advocacy efforts
2010-2015 Grassroots Leader: Expanded programs, built partnerships
2015-Present National Advocate: Gained recognition, influenced policy and global awareness

Lessons Learned

My journey has taught me that leadership is about perseverance, empathy, and collaboration. From educator to activist, I've grown through every experience. This path continues to inspire me as I work towards a more equitable Kenya. In the next post, I'll share the impact and achievements that have resulted from this journey.
